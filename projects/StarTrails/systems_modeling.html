Systems Modeling

After researching and studying the different models and the benefits they encompass, we decided to implement the State-Transition Diagram and an ER (Entity-Relational) Diagram to model fundamentally crucial aspects of our application.

Entity-Relational Diagram

The ER Diagram will allow us to model what our database should look like and how the data should behave. ER Diagrams are useful in complex database systems as it allows developers a high-level “Top-Down” view of their database. ER Diagrams contain different symbols to represent the different types of data and relationships included in the database. Rectangles represent different entities or tables, ovals are used to represent the attributes of a table, and a diamond shape is for representing the relationships between tables.

We broke our data down into 6 different “entities”: User, Site, Review, Event, Amenity, and Map. All of these entities have a unique primary key specified by the underlined attribute and different relationships each entity participates in, specified by the red diamond. Each entity also includes attributes related to what those entities are representing. For instance, the Map entity has a name, id, and an attribute that accepts multiple data images. Allowing the one map overlay to have different images stored.

State Transition Diagram

The State-Transition Diagram will help our team and relevant stakeholders visualize and manage the behavior of our vast array of screens.The transitions of our system are transitions between screens, system functions like adding a review to a site, toggling the map filters, or bookmarking a site, and basic operations like logging in, or signing out.

This State Network Diagram shows the navigational flow from each screen to the others. Our Map section is the most connected area of the diagram. This is because the map screen is intended to be our “Wow” Factor. We chose to include the state transition model for our project because with the large number of states our system will go through, as seen in our proposed use cases from our Low-Fidelity Prototype, laying out the flow and observing the different states on the diagram can help us to improve the flow of our system so it can be more intuitive for a user and also allow us to see any missing functionalities or redundancies that can be removed from our system.
